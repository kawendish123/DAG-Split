# ABSTRACT

Mobile edge computing is conducive to artificial intelligence computing near terminals, in which Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) should be partitioned to allocate tasks partially to the edge for execution to reduce latency and save energy. Most of the existing studies assume that the tasks are of the same type or the computing resources of the server are the same. In real life, Mobile Devices (MDs) and Edge Servers (ESs) are heterogeneous in type and computing resources, it is challenging to find the optimal partition point for each DNN and offload it to an appropriate ES. To fill this gap, we propose a partitioning-and-offloading scheme for the heterogeneous tasks-server system to reduce the overall system latency and energy consumption on DNN inference. The scheme has four steps. First, it establishes a partitioning and task offloading model for adaptive DNN model. Second, to reduce the solution space, the scheme designs a Partition Point Retain (PPR) algorithm. After that, the scheme gives an Optimal Partition Point (OPP) Algorithm to find the optimal partition point with the minimum cost for each ES corresponding to each MD. Based on the partition points, an offloading of DNN tasks for each MD is presented to finish the whole scheme. Simulations show that the proposed scheme reduces the total cost by $77.9\%$ and $59.9\%$ on average compared to Only-Local and Only-Server respectively in the heterogeneous edge computing environment. 

# 1. Introduction

Deep learning methods based on Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) outperform traditional object recognition and detection methods in artificial intelligence areas such as computer vision [1], natural language processing [2], and speech recognition [3]. However, deep learning is characterized with the high dimensionality of input data and millions of computations. Fig. 1 gives such an example of DNN model multilayer neural networks and massive data which is just recognizing a cat picture. A common approach to meet the computational demands of deep learning is to put them on the cloud [4]. However, this approach leads to higher latency due to the distance, which is unacceptable for real-time interactive applications such as internet of vehicle [5,6] and augmented reality [7]. Also, it will lead to poor scalability with the increasing number of connected Mobile Devices (MDs). 

Fortunately, mobile edge computing [8] can address the above-mentioned problems in terms of latency [9], scalability, and privacy challenges [10]. Multiple Edge Servers (ESs) with computation resources are placed near MDs [11], and each MD can decide whether to offload a portion of its DNN tasks to the ES. Hence the DNN for each task will be partitioned and offloaded to divide the DNN model into two 

parts, one is to be executed on the MD and the other is to be offloaded for ES execution [12]. Fig. 2(a) shows the traditional solution under the scenario with one MD, one DNN task, and one ES. 

However, in real life, scenes involve a lot of complex issues. As shown in Fig. 2(b), there might be multiple MDs and multiple ESs each of which has different DNN tasks such as image or speech recognition. Also, MDs and ESs such as smart devices, base stations, and gateways have different computing capabilities, which implies the heterogeneity in the MDs and ESs in computing capabilities. We call this scenario the heterogeneous edge computing scenario. Therefore, to serve more realistic edge computing scenarios, DNN model partitioning and task offloading should be jointly considered in the heterogeneous edge computing scenario. 

In this paper, we study the model partitioning and offloading optimization in DNN-based tasks under heterogeneous edge computing scenario. The main contributions of this work are as follows. 

1. We build a system model for approximating heterogeneous edge computing scenario, which takes DNN model partitioning and task offloading into consideration, and takes DNN inference latency and energy consumption as optimization objectives.

# 2. Related work

This section discusses the work related to the deployment of deep learning services in the edge environment from three aspects: DNN model compression and partition, transmission data compression and early exit, resource management and task offloading. 

# 2.1. DNN model compression and partition

Deep learning applications require a large number of computational resources, which are insufficient on MDs [13]. To enable DNN models to be deployed on resource-constrained devices, some studies aim to make lighter DNN models. For example, in [14], the authors proposed methods to compress deep neural networks with pruning, training quantization, and Huffman coding and make DNN models easier to deploy on resource-limited devices. In [15,16], the authors proposed to design lightweight DNN models by improving the structure of network models. 

Rational partitioning of deep learning models plays a crucial role in the deployment of intelligent services in edge environments. Currently, deep learning model partitioning can be classified into vertical partitioning [17], horizontal partitioning [18,19], and combined horizontal-vertical partitioning [20,21]. In [17], the authors proposed Neurosurgeon, an end-cloud collaborative computing scheme based on layer-granularity model partitioning. It deploys neural networks to MDs and cloud computing centers with layer granularity partitioning, and adaptively partitions models based on network bandwidth, with the device and cloud collaborating on model forward inference tasks. In [22], the authors proposed DeepThings, which partitions the convolutional operations of a neural network vertically by fusing earlier convolutional layers and distributes the partitioned computational tasks to different edge devices to compute model inference tasks in parallel.

# 2.3. Resource management and task offloading

Resource management and task offloading have been extensively researched in edge computing environments. Task offloading can reduce the computational burden on MDs by shifting some tasks from resource-limited MDs to servers [32-34]. The authors minimized task processing latency while avoiding network congestion by scheduling tasks from end-users that cannot be processed by ESs to a remote cloud for processing [35]. To reduce the reliance on remote clouds, the authors proposed a distributed task co-processing approach based on graph staining and Gibbs sampling theory to minimize task processing cost overhead [36]. The authors considered a multi-user and multi-server scenario and proposed an efficient multi-objective evolutionary algorithm that addresses the problems of minimizing response time, energy consumption, and cost [37]. In [38,39], the authors investigated pricing-driven offloading schemes to rationalize the use of limited computational resources in the server by charging for the subtasks offloaded from each MD according to the server's resource allocation. 

However, most existing offloading schemes do not scale to support DNN tasks in edge computing networks. To address these issues, we investigate DNN task partitioning and offloading strategies for multiple users in edge computing networks. Gao et al. [23] designed a slot model and a dynamic pricing policy to efficiently schedule offloading DNN subtasks. Tang et al. [24] proposed an algorithm called iterative alternating optimization (IAO) to solve the DNN partitioning problem under multi-user resource constraints. Dong et al. [25] considered the 

shortage of computing resources and high rental costs at the edge, and proposed a constrained stochastic optimization approach to achieve computational efficiency of local devices and cost efficiency of edge clouds. However, the above three methods only consider offloading DNN tasks from multiple MDs to one ES, and as the number of DNN tasks to be offloaded becomes larger, it is easy to cause problems such as low processing efficiency of ESs and unfavorable utilization of computational resources of surrounding idle ESs. Shi et al. [26] proposed an offline algorithm GSPI and an online algorithm to accelerate DNN inference and considered DNN tasks of multiple MDs offloaded to multiple ESs, but the DNN task for each MD is the same and only optimizes the inference latency, while users in realistic scenarios often have different DNN tasks. Table 1 shows the comparison between this paper and the above five literatures in terms of task scenarios, DNN models used, and optimization objectives. 

Most of the work focused on DNN model partitioning and offloading in single-task and single-server or multi-task and single-server scenarios. However, heterogeneous edge computing scenarios with multiple ESs, multiple MDs and multiple different DNN tasks must be considered when using DNN model partitioning and offloading in real edge computing environments. No previous work considered this realistic task scenario, which is the focus of our work. We need to consider how to partition DNN tasks of multiple MDs and offload them to appropriate ESs. In addition, adaptive changes in DNN model partitioning and offloading in dynamic environments need to be considered. In this paper, we jointly study DNN model partitioning and task offloading in heterogeneous edge computing scenarios to reduce system latency and energy consumption for DNN inference. 

# 3. System model

In this section, we design the system model and establish a model with global latency and energy consumption as the optimization objectives. As shown in Fig. 2(b), considering a two-dimensional network consisting of ESs and MDs, $M$ represents a set MDs, $m_{i}$ ( $m_{i} \in M, i = 1 \dots m$ ) represents one of the MDs, $S$ represents a set of ESs, and $s_{i}$ ( $s_{i} \in S, i = 1 \dots s$ ) represents one of the ESs. ESs and MDs are heterogeneous, which means that each ES and MD has different computing resources. Each MD is equipped with a well-trained DNN model. $L$ represents the set of DNN layers number of each MD, and $l_{i}$ ( $l_{i} \in L, i = 1 \dots m$ ) represents the DNN layers of a MD. Our task scenario is that multiple MDs with different DNN tasks need to be offloaded to multiple ESs at the same time. Table 2 lists the main symbols used in this article. 

# 3.1. DNN partitioning

DNN consists of multiple layers such as convolutional layers, activation layers, and fully connected layers. We assume that the DNN used by $m_{i}$ has $l_{i}$ layers. Let $m_{i}^{j}, j \in \{1,2,\dots,l_{i}\}$ denote the set of a layer index. For the DNN forward inference process, the inputs are computed at each layer in turn from left to right to obtain the final output, where the output of the previous layer is the input of the next layer. It can be seen that there is data dependency between the DNN layers, but that each layer is different since the size of the computation and the size of the output data are different for each layer and the computations do not interfere with each other, which provides an opportunity for DNN partition [17].

Fig. 2(a) shows the partition and offloading of the DNN model under the edge computing network, where there is only one partition point and the divided part is offloaded to the ES. Assuming that $p*$ is the partition point of the DNN, the MD performs the computation from layer 1 to layer $p*$ of the DNN, the output of layer $p*$ is transmitted to the ES via the edge computing network, the ES performs the computation from $(p* + 1)$ to the output layer of the DNN, and the final inference result is transmitted back to the MD. In particular, if $p* = l_i$ , the whole task is computed locally at the MD, and if $p* = 0$ , then the whole task is offloaded to the ES. 

To speed up the execution process of DNN inference tasks, each MD is able to make cascading offloading decisions using the DNN partitioning approach described above, i.e., the DNN in each MD is partitioned into two parts. The DNN front-end part is executed on the user's MD, and the DNN back-end part is offloaded to any ES in the system for execution, as in Fig. 2(b). It is assumed that all MDs can connect directly to all ESs through a wireless network in this region and DNN tasks of MDs need to be processed at the same time. We determine the partition point and computation servers for DNN tasks for each MD, so that all tasks can be computed as quickly as possible and with minimum total cost of all MDs. 

# 3.2. DNN execution delay and energy consumption model

Before calculating the execution delay of DNN, we need to know the computation and output data size of each layer of the DNN model. We consider the computation of each layer as FLoating point OperationS (FLOPs), which represents the number of basic mathematical operations (such as addition, subtraction, multiplication, etc.) to be performed. The size of the parametric quantity of the fully connected layer is $(d_{in} + 1)d_{out}$ , where $d_{in}$ denotes the dimension of the input feature vector, $+1$ denotes the bias, and $d_{out}$ denotes the output dimension. FLOPs of the fully connected layer is: 

$$
\boldsymbol {F} _ {L} = \left[ d _ {i n} + \left(d _ {i n} - 1\right) + 1 \right] d _ {\text {o u t}}, \tag {1}
$$

where $d_{in}$ denotes the multiplicative operation, $(d_{in} - 1)$ denotes the additive operation, $+1$ denotes the bias, and $d_{out}$ denotes the output dimension. 

The parameter quantity of convolutional layer is $(k_w k_h c_{in} + 1)c_{out}$ , where $k_w, k_h$ denotes the width and height of a convolution kernel, $+1$ denotes the bias, and $c_{out}$ number of convolution kernels. FLOPs of the convolutional layers is: 

$$
\boldsymbol {F} _ {C} = \left[ \left(c _ {i n} k _ {w} k _ {h}\right) + \left(c _ {i n} k _ {w} k _ {h} - 1\right) + 1 \right] c _ {\text {o u t}} w h, \tag {2}
$$

where the value in parentheses represents the amount of computation required for convolution to calculate a point in the feature map (multiplication + addition). $(c_{in}k_wk_h)$ denotes the multiplication in one convolution operation, $(c_{in}k_wk_h - 1)$ denotes the addition in one convolution operation, $+1$ denotes the bias, $w$ and $h$ denotes the width and height of the feature map respectively. $(c_{out}wh)$ denotes the number of all elements of the feature map. The output data size of each layer is: 

$$
\boldsymbol {D} _ {L} = c _ {i n} w h * 4 / 1 0 2 4 / 1 0 2 4, \tag {3}
$$

where $c_{in}$ is the number of channels of the input feature map, $w$ and $h$ are the width and height of the feature map. Assuming that the tensor size of the input image is $(3 \times 224 \times 224)$ , the computation and output data size of each layer of the AlexNet [40] model are shown in Fig. 3. From the figure, it can be seen that each layer in the AlexNet model has different computation and output data size.

DNN computation cost consists of DNN inference latency and energy consumption. DNN inference latency consists of DNN computation time in MD and ES and data transmission time, where data transmission time is affected by network bandwidth. DNN inference energy consumption consists of DNN computation energy consumption in MD and transmission energy consumption. 

Once a DNN partitioning decision has been made, the execution time of a DNN will consist of four components, namely the local execution time of the front-end part of the DNN, the transmission time of the intermediate data transfer to the ES, the execution time of the back-end part of the DNN at the ES and the time of the inference results back to the MD, which is described as follows. 

1. Execution time on MD: Let $X_{i,p_i}$ denotes the local computation of the DNN task of MD $m_i$ before the partition point $p_i$ ; let $F_i^{local}$ denotes the computational capacity of MD $m_i$ . The local execution time is: 

$$
\boldsymbol {T} _ {i} ^ {\text {l o c a l}} = X _ {i, p _ {i}} / F _ {i} ^ {\text {l o c a l}} = \sum_ {j = 1} ^ {p _ {i}} \mathrm {C m} _ {i} ^ {j} / F _ {i} ^ {\text {l o c a l}}, \tag {4}
$$

where $C m_{i}^{j}$ denotes the computation of layer $m_{i}^{j}$ , $\sum_{j=1}^{pi} \mathrm{Cm}_{i}^{j}$ denotes the sum of the computation of all layers between the first and the partition layer $p_{i}$ of the DNN model on $m_{i}$ . 

2. Transmission time of intermediate output data: Let $Dm_{i}^{j}(j = p_{i})$ denotes the size of the output data to be transmitted after DNN partition; let $B_{ul}$ denotes the uplink bandwidth of the edge computing network. The transmission time of intermediate data transmission to the ES is: 

$$
T _ {i} ^ {u p} = D m _ {i} ^ {p _ {i}} / B _ {u l}. \tag {5}
$$

3. Execution time on the ES: let $Y_{i,p_i}$ denotes the amount of edge computation for the DNN task of MD $m_i$ after the partition point $p_i$ ; let $F_{i,j}^{es}$ denotes the computational resources allocated to $m_i$ by ES $s_j$ , where $0 < F_{i,j}^{es} \leq F_{j}^{es}$ . The ES execution time is: 

$$
\boldsymbol {T} _ {i, j} ^ {e s} = Y _ {i, p _ {i}} / F _ {i, j} ^ {e s} = \sum_ {j = p _ {i}} ^ {l _ {i}} C m _ {i} ^ {j} / F _ {i, j} ^ {e s}, \tag {6}
$$

where $\sum_{j = p_i}^{l_i}Cm_i^j$ denotes the sum of the computation between the layer after the partition layer $p_i$ and the last layer of the DNN model on $m_{i}$ 

4. Time when the inference result is returned to the MD: let $Dm_i^{l_i}$ indicate the data size of the final inference result; let $B_{down}$ to indicate the downlink bandwidth of edge computing network. The time when the inference result is returned to the MD is: 

$$
\boldsymbol {T} _ {i} ^ {\text {d o w n}} = D m _ {i} ^ {l _ {i}} / B _ {\text {d o w n}}. \tag {7}
$$

5. Thus the total execution time of a MD $m_{i}$ after partition a DNN task is the sum of the execution time on the MD, the intermediate output transfer time, the execution time on the ES and the time of the inference results back to the $m_{i}$ : 

$$
\boldsymbol {T} _ {i} = T _ {i} ^ {\text {l o c a l}} + T _ {i} ^ {u p} + T _ {i, j} ^ {e s} + T _ {i} ^ {d o w n}. \tag {8}
$$

Once a DNN partitioning decision is made, the energy consumption of a MD will have two components, the energy consumption for performing DNN computations locally, and the transmission energy consumption for transmitting intermediate data, which is described as follows: 

1. Execution energy consumption on MD: Assume that $P_{i}^{local}$ is the computational power of $m_{i}$ device and $T_{i}^{local}$ denotes the local computation time. The local execution energy consumption is: 

$$
\boldsymbol {E} _ {i} ^ {\text {l o c a l}} = P _ {i} ^ {\text {l o c a l}} T _ {i} ^ {\text {l o c a l}}. \tag {9}
$$

2. The transmission energy consumption of intermediate output data: assume that $P_{i}^{up}$ is the upload power and $T_{i}^{up}$ is the DNN model transmission data time of $m_{i}$ . Then the transmission energy consumption is: 

$$
\boldsymbol {E} _ {\mathrm {i}} ^ {u p} = \boldsymbol {P} _ {i} ^ {u p} \boldsymbol {T} _ {i} ^ {u p}. \tag {10}
$$

3. Thus the total energy consumption for the execution of a MD $m_{i}$ partitioning DNN task is the sum of the energy consumption for execution on the MD and the energy consumption for intermediate output transfers: 

$$
\boldsymbol {E} _ {i} = E _ {i} ^ {\text {l o c a l}} + E _ {i} ^ {\text {u p}}. \tag {11}
$$

# 3.3. Problem definition

In this work, we want to optimize the inference latency and energy consumption by finding the optimal partition point of DNN layers and offloading some DNN layers to the appropriate ES for execution. The total cost of all MDs is: 

$$
C _ {t o t a l} = \alpha \sum_ {i = 1} ^ {m} T _ {i} + \beta \sum_ {i = 1} ^ {m} E _ {i}. \tag {12}
$$

The cost of DNN execution per MD is the time cost and energy cost, and $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are weighting factors. Our goal is to minimize $C_{total}$ , i.e., the sum of the costs per MD. Each DNN can only be offloaded to one ES for execution, and one ES can execute multiple DNN tasks in parallel. 

# 4. Algorithm design

Before finding solutions to the problem formulated, we first do some preparatory work. PPR is hence proposed to reduce the complexity of the subsequent algorithms. Then, a quantization and compression method [29] is used to reduce the latency of transmitted data. This work will optimize the data transmission delay without affecting the accuracy performance of the DNN model. 

After the preparatory work, we propose an OPP algorithm to find the optimal partition point with the minimum cost for each MD's DNN. Then, a best matching algorithm is designed to partition and offload the DNN model of each MD to the appropriate ES. 

# 4.1. The PPR algorithm

Each DNN layer can be used as a partition point, which results in a particularly large solution space. However, some of the layers in DNN are not suitable as potential partition points since their output data is too large, which will incur high communication costs. In this case, we need to design an algorithm in the polynomial to eliminate these layers and reduce the solution space, we call this algorithm Partition Point Retain (PPR) algorithm. Before partitioning and offloading the DNN model, we can use the PPR algorithm in advance to eliminate the layers that may not be partition points. We need to set a time threshold for each DNN model to determine the final candidate partition point. If the time after the partition of layers is less than the time threshold, the layer will be confirmed as the candidate partition point, otherwise, it will be excluded. Thus, we will arrive at a suitable set of partition point $RP$ for each DNN model. See Algorithm 1 for detailed steps.

Line 2 of Algorithm 1 is to update each layer of the DNN model, the computation and output data size per layer, and the number of layers. Line 5 is to calculate the execution time after partitioning each layer. Line 6 is to calculate the average execution time of the partition layers. Line 8 is to determine the size of the current layer execution time with respect to the standard time threshold, and if it is small, it is added to the set of alternative layers under the corresponding bandwidth. Otherwise, the current layer is not considered as a partition 

point. Line 16 is to output the final alternative partition layers for each DNN model. 

Data transmission latency, which accounts for a large percentage of the model inference latency, is optimized by using a near-lossless compression scheme [29], which converts 32-bit floating-point neurons to 8-bit byte types, thereby reducing the data size by a factor of nearly four. Due to the fault-tolerance of neural network inference, the compression scheme will not lose the accuracy of the model when the category corresponding to the maximum value of the output component is the same as the result of the uncompressed scheme. The data transmission latency is reduced by this scheme. 

# 4.2. The OPP algorithm

By finding the optimal partition point for each MD's DNN model can reduce its inference latency. Since each MD and ES have different computing capabilities, the DNN tasks of different MDs are offloaded to different ESs with different partition points, and it is necessary to find the optimal partition point for each MD corresponding to the ES. We determine the optimal partition point based on calculating the cost of DNN inference according to Formula (12). Finally, the cost matrix is derived, and the elements of the matrix are the combination of minimum cost and optimal partition point. The specific steps are described in Algorithm 2.

The purpose of OPP (Algorithm 2) is to find the optimal partition point of DNN tasks in each MD corresponding to each ES. The optimal partition point is the layer that finds the least cost among the alternative layers derived from Algorithm 1. Line 1 in Algorithm 2 indicates traversing the MDs, and line 2 indicates updating the attributes of the DNN in the MD, including the output data size and computation per layer and the number of layers. Lines 8-12 determine whether the cost is minimum and the minimum is the optimal partition point, otherwise, it returns to step 4, where line 9 adds the minimum cost and the optimal partition point to the cost matrix. Finally, line 13 indicates the cost matrix. 

# 4.3. Best matching algorithm

The best matching solution is found based on the cost matrix obtained from OPP, i.e., the $m * m$ cost matrix of DNN tasks of all MDs offloaded to each ES. The DNN tasks of each MD are offloaded to one ES to minimize the global cost. If the exhaustive method is used, the complexity of the algorithm will be very high, reaching $O(n!)$ . The Hungarian algorithm [41] can be used for this problem, which greatly reduces the complexity of the algorithm and the complexity becomes $O(n^{3})$ . The purpose of the algorithm is to find the best matching solution, i.e., to find the best offloaded ES for each MD's DNN task. 

# 4.4. Complexity analysis

In this subsection the complexity of these three algorithms is analyzed, and since we are only interested in how fast the algorithms are executed, only the time complexity is discussed here. First, we analyze the PPR algorithm, which is preparatory work, so it can be executed offline, and the time generated by the PPR algorithm will not be counted in the total time complexity. Suppose $u$ DNN models are used in the PPR algorithm, the DNN model has $v$ layers, and the number of bandwidths tested is $k$ . The result $u * v * k$ can be calculated, which can be considered as a constant. So the time complexity of the PPR algorithm is $O(1)$ . 

The OPP algorithm results in a cost matrix, and the original process requires traversing each layer of the DNN model of each MD and the ESs, which causes a large time complexity. However, by preparing the PPR algorithm, only the candidate layers obtained by the PPR algorithm need to be traversed instead of traversing each layer, which can reduce the time complexity of the OPP algorithm, so the time complexity of the OPP algorithm is $O(n^{2})$ . Finally, the best matching algorithm is analyzed. After the cost matrix is obtained by OPP algorithm, a global matching result with minimum cost needs to be obtained. If we use the exhaustive method to match, this time complexity is $O(n!)$ , which is unacceptable, here we use the Hungarian algorithm, so the time complexity of the best matching algorithm becomes $O(n^{3})$ .

# 6. Conclusion and future work

# 6.1. Conclusion

In this paper, we study the multi-user DNN model partitioning and task offloading problem in heterogeneous edge computing scenarios. We perform the partitioning and offloading of DNNs for each user with the goal of minimizing the total cost of DNN inference for all users. Our solution is divided into the following steps: first, we build a system model with multiple MDs, multiple DNN tasks, and multiple ESs. Then, to reduce the selection of DNN partition points, we propose the Partition Point Retain (PPR) algorithm. Next, based on layer partitioning, we propose an Optimal Partition Point (OPP) algorithm and best matching algorithm to implement DNN model partitioning and task offloading for each user. Finally, we experimentally demonstrate that our proposed scheme can guarantee the smallest total cost of DNN inference for all users in the heterogeneous edge computing scenario, with a $77.9\%$ and $55.9\%$ reduction in total cost compared to the Only-Local and Only-Server schemes, respectively. Our scheme is applicable to the case of using different DNN models for partitioning and offloading in computing devices with multiple different computing capabilities, and it can adapt to dynamic network environments. We believe that our research can contribute to the further development of edge intelligence and can provide a reference for multi-user DNN partitioning and offloading research. 

# 6.2. Future work

Several aspects can be further explored to enhance the performance of the proposed multi-user DNN partitioning and offloading scheme for future work. Three aspects are listed and discussed as follows: 



1. ES Rental Cost: We calculate the time and energy consumption of DNN inference as the computational cost of DNN, but the rental cost of the ES is also the computational cost of DNN. Since the pricing model of the ES is not mature, we do not include the ES cost when determining the total cost of DNN inference. We will include ES rental cost in future work to further optimize the quality of user experience 





2. Early Exit: Early exit technique is one of the key techniques for DNN partition, but using the early exit technique causes loss of inference accuracy. We consider using the early exit technique in our future work, we try to consider the trade-off between inference latency and inference accuracy to give priority to user experience quality and exit early or complete all inference according to user demand. 





3. Server Waiting Time: Our task scenario is to process multiple users' DNN tasks at the same time and the ES is idle state. In future work, we will consider the DNN task queuing of users and the waiting time of the ES
